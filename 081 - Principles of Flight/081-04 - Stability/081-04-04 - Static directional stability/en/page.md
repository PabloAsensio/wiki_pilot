# Directional Stability


## Mechanism
Directional stability is provided primarily by the **Vertical Fin (Vertical Stabilizer)**.
*   If the aircraft yaws (sideslips), the fin meets the airflow at an angle of attack.
*   This generates a sideways aerodynamic force (lift) on the fin.
*   Since the fin is behind the CG, this force creates a moment that rotates the nose back into the wind (**Weathercock Effect**).

## Factors Affecting Directional Stability
1.  **Fin Area**: Larger fin = Greater stability.
2.  **Fin Moment Arm**: Distance from CG to Fin. Longer = Greater stability.
3.  **Sweepback**: Increases directional stability. A swept wing advancing into the wind produces more drag than the retreating wing, creating a restoring moment.
4.  **Keel Area**: Area of fuselage/fin behind the CG aids stability. Area ahead of the CG (long nose) destabilizes.

## Dorsal / Ventral Fins
Additional surfaces added to increase keel area aft of the CG to prevent directional instability (e.g., at high angles of attack or due to fuselage blanking).
