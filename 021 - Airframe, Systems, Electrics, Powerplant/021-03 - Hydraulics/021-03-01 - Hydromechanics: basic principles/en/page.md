**Introduction**

Hydraulic systems are the "muscle" of modern aircraft, critical for operating heavy machinery and flight controls. By using fluid under pressure to transmit force, these systems allow pilots to move large components with precision and reliability, even under high aerodynamic loads.

## Function and Purpose

The primary function of the hydraulic system is to **move and actuate critical components**. Hydraulic systems are essential because they provide:

- **High Force:** A relatively small pump can generate high pressure that translates into immense force at the actuator.
- **Smooth Control:** Hydraulic fluid is virtually incompressible, offering precise and smooth movement.
- **Reliability:** Systems can be routed easily to remote locations and offer redundancy for safety-critical functions.

## Main Consumers

The biggest "users" or consumers of hydraulic power are **actuators**, which are devices that convert hydraulic pressure into mechanical movement. The two most significant systems powered by hydraulics are:

1. **Flight Controls:** Primary and secondary surfaces (ailerons, elevators, rudder, flaps, spoilers).
2. **Landing Gear:** Extension/retraction mechanisms, brakes, and steering.

*Note: Systems like hydro-mechanical fuel control units, fuel-cooled oil coolers, or constant speed propellers typically do not use the main aircraft hydraulic system (propellers often use engine oil pressure).*

## Basic Principles

Hydraulics act as a means of force multiplication and transmission.

- **Pascal’s Law:** The working principle of hydraulic systems is based on Pascal’s law of pressure transmission. A force exerted on a confined fluid is transmitted equally in all directions, allowing a piston at one end to actuate an output mechanism at the other.
- **Pressure:** Defined as the force per unit area exerted on a surface.
    - **Formula:** $Pressure = \frac{Force}{Surface Area}$
