Understanding how an aircraft is designed and maintained is fundamental to aviation safety. This section covers the strict operational procedures, critical structural design concepts like safe-life and fail-safe, the vital role of redundancy, and the essential certification specifications that ensure every aircraft is built to fly safely.

## Operating Procedures and Instructions (EASA AIR OPS)
According to **EASA AIR OPS** (specifically AMC1 SPA.LVO.125), an operator must specify detailed operating procedures and instructions in their operations or procedures manual. Crucially, these instructions must be compatible with the limitations and mandatory procedures contained in the **Airplane Flight Manual (AFM)**. 

The manual must cover a wide range of specific items:
- Checks for the satisfactory functioning of aircraft equipment (both pre-flight and in-flight).
- The effect on minima caused by changes in the status of ground installations and airborne equipment.
- Procedures for every phase of flight: take-off, approach, flare, hover, landing, rollout, and missed approach.
- Actions to be followed in the event of failures, warnings (including HUD/HUDLS/EVS), and other non-normal situations.
- Minimum visual reference required, the actions to take if visual reference deteriorates, and the importance of correct seating and eye position.
- Clear allocation of crew duties to allow the pilot-in-command to focus mainly on supervision and decision making.
- Mandatory rules such as basing height calls below 200 ft on the radio altimeter, continuous instrument monitoring by one pilot until landing, and protecting the localiser sensitive area.
- Use of weather information like wind velocity, wind shear, turbulence, runway contamination, and multiple RVR assessments.
- Specific procedures for LTS CAT I, OTS CAT II, EVS approaches, and practice approaches.
- Operating limitations resulting from airworthiness certification, and information on the maximum allowable deviation from the ILS glide path and/or localiser.

## Structural Design Concepts
Structural design concepts are developed to ensure the safe operation of aircraft components by managing how parts handle regular wear, cyclical stress, and potential failure.

### Safe-Life Design
In a **Safe-Life** design, the component is built so that no catastrophic failure will occur during a specified minimum lifespan. The life of such a structure can be defined by factors like the number of cycles, flight hours, pressurization events, or simply calendar time. Crucially, in a safe-life design, **one load path is sufficient**, but the part must be **replaced, strictly inspected, or overhauled** at the end of its declared lifetime, regardless of its apparent physical condition.

### Fail-Safe Design
In a **Fail-Safe** design, if a primary structural element fails (such as cracking), the design includes built-in **redundancy**. This means there is an alternative or **multiple load paths** provided by adjacent parts that can take over the structural loads. It allows the structure to withstand some weakening without catastrophic failure, but only for a **limited amount of time**. This time is usually designed to be long enough to safely reach the next scheduled periodic inspection, where the damage will be identified and the part repaired or replaced. 

## The Concept of Redundancy
**Redundancy** is the core principle underlying the fail-safe design concept. Redundancy means "additional" or "duplicate", ensuring there is not just one load path or one source for an essential system. 
- For **structures**, redundancy involves multiple load paths so that if one member breaks, the others can carry the required forces. 
- For **systems**, redundancy means having extra sources (such as duplicate electrical power or hydraulic pressure) to prevent a complete failure of vital systems like flight controls or landing gear extension.

## Certification Specifications
All aircraft must be certified according to rules determined by competent aviation authorities (like EASA). **Certification Specifications (CS)** are non-binding technical standards adopted to meet essential safety requirements. These CSs are used to establish the **Certification Basis (CB)** of an aircraft. They lay down the strict requirements covering various aspects, mainly focusing on the **design, material quality, build quality, and behavioral characteristics** of the aircraft.
