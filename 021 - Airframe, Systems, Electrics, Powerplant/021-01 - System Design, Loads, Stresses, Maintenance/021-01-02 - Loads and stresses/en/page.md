Understanding the loads and stresses that act upon an aircraft is critical to flight safety. From the moment an aircraft is parked on the ground to when it performs complex maneuvers, its structural components undergo continuous physical forces. This section provides an exhaustive look into types of loads, how stress and strain behave, and the operational limitations pilots must respect to ensure the airframe's longevity.

## Stress and Strain Core Concepts
**Stress** is the internal force per unit of cross-sectional area generated inside a structural part as a result of external loads. It is expressed in Pascals (Pa), which is equal to Force divided by Area. Stress can take several forms, including:
- **Tension** (tensile stress resulting from stretching)
- **Compression** (resulting from squashing)
- **Torsion** (a twisting form of stress)
- **Shearing** (when components slide against each other)
Note that **traction** is a mechanical force intended to put something in motion.

**Strain** is the physical deformation or change in size (measured as *Change in Size / Original Size*) caused by the action of stress reaching a specific magnitude. Simply put, any force increase causes an increase in stress, and any stress increase causes an increase in strain.

These forces are always present. Even when parked, the aircraft's weight rests on the landing gear and load-bearing structures. Gravity pulls the heavy wings downward, putting the top surface under tension and the bottom under compression. Conversely, during straight and level flight, the wings generate lift and flex upwards, reversing the forces: the top surface is under compression, while the bottom is under tension. The amount of stress a component endures determines whether the resulting deformation is temporary (**elastic**) or permanent (**plastic**).

## Types of Loads on an Airframe
Loads on an aircraft happen constantly, both in the air and on the ground.
- **Static loads**: Forces that do not change in size, position, or direction (e.g., the aircraft's weight through the wheels while parked, or static tension on the wings).
- **Dynamic loads**: Forces that change in size, position, or direction (e.g., turbulence bending the wing spar, taxiing loads, or moving control surfaces).
- **Cyclic loads**: A special type of dynamic, reversible load involving repeated or fluctuating stresses over the structure's life cycle. Examples include the fuselage expanding and contracting during **pressurization cycles** per flight, wind gust fluctuations, or the impact of touchdowns. Such repetitive forces lead to fatigue degradation.

## Design Limits and Load Factors
An airplane's **limit load** (or limit load factor) is the maximum load it is expected to experience during flight operations. Aircraft must be able to withstand **150% of this limit load** before structural failure occurs; this extreme threshold is known as the **ultimate load**. For example, CS-25 transport aircraft have a minimum limit load factor of 2.5G. This means they are tested to endure up to 3.75G without failure. Exceeding the limit load is not recommended to avoid irreversible airframe bending, unless necessary for safety.

When an aircraft maneuvers, such as entering a level turn or increasing bank angle, it must produce more lift, thereby increasing the wings' load factor and placing extra stress on the wing spars and roots. As the required lift and load factor increase with a steeper bank angle, the safe margin between the current load factor and the maximum allowed load factor naturally decreases. 
It is important to remember that in level and unaccelerated flight, the load factor is always 1, whether flaps are extended or in a clean configuration. However, when extending the flaps, the aircraft's maximum allowed limit load is lower. This is not because the wing structure becomes weaker, but because the locking mechanisms and moving parts of the flaps cannot sustain the same forces as the solid wing itself.

## Critical Stress Areas and Inspections
To maintain safety and ensure cyclic load limitations are not exceeded, pilots must keep a log of every landing. Pre-flight inspections must be thorough, particularly regarding components designed to be thin yet strong (outer skins, longerons, frames).
- **Wing root and spars**: The maximum bending moment occurs at the wing root. Any deformation (excessive strain) may cause cracking, sometimes visible on the wing root fairings.
- **Fuselage skin and pressure bulkheads**: Differential pressure between the inside and outside of the aircraft causes immense hoop stresses. The aft pressure bulkhead and the areas around the windows are highly sensitive.
- **Top and bottom fuselage skin**: Must be meticulously checked after a heavy landing or high negative G-forces. For instance, if an aircraft exceeds its vertical descent velocity and touches down too hard, the loads transmitted from the landing gear can bend the wings downwards, placing the bottom under compression, the top under tension, and shearing structural rivets.
- **Skin wrinkles and control surfaces**: Thin metal skin panels under excessive stress may buckle, visibly appearing as "wrinkles" under proper lighting. Control surfaces must be tested physically for unrestricted motion, as damage or icing can limit their movement. Additionally, inspecting the tail skid is crucial for spotting evidence of tailstrikes.
