Fatigue and corrosion are two of the most significant natural threats to an aircraft's structural integrity over time. Understanding how the environment and operational cycles degrade materials is essential for proper maintenance and flight safety.

## Metal Fatigue
**Fatigue** is a condition induced in materials due to **cyclic loads**, such as the repetitive pressurization cycles of the fuselage or the flexing of wings. When an aircraft structure experiences repeated or fluctuating stresses (reversal of loading), the metal gradually weakens. Ultimately, fatigue can cause the material to fracture even when subjected to a stress or force much smaller than would normally cause a single-application failure. 
- Because fatigue is tied to structural stress cycles, an aircraft performing many short flights a day (for example, six flights of 30 minutes each) will accrue more cyclic loads and reach its life limit much faster than an aircraft flying the same total duration but fewer flights. 
- Finding fatigue requires a mechanic to perform **visual inspections during scheduled maintenance**. Systems like HUMS (Health and Usage Monitoring Systems) track component usage, and magnetic chip detectors find metal inside engine oil, but they do not automatically detect fatigue on the airframe.
- Signs of continuous stress or impending failure include **visible cracking** (stresses exceeding the material's yield strength) and buckling/wrinkling (indicating massive compression). Cosmetic issues like faded paint or scratches do not inherently indicate continuous structural stress. In general, "ageing" of a plane brings scratches, dents, and dirt which increases **skin friction drag** because they disrupt laminar airflow over the aircraft body.

## The Threat of Corrosion
**Corrosion** is the deterioration of a metal in a specific environment resulting from a chemical breakdown when two reactive substances mix. The rate of this corrosion depends heavily on five main factors:
- **Temperature**: An increase of 10°C typically doubles the corrosion rate.
- **Humidity** and **Rainfall**: Higher atmospheric moisture significantly increases the rate of corrosion.
- **Sulphur Dioxide (pollution)**: Acidic contaminants and pollutants accelerate material damage.
- **Air Salinity**: Salt acts as an electrolyte. Oceanic/marine environments at low altitudes are highly corrosive, causing massive salt build-up on the airframe and engines.
Consequently, an aircraft flying in **tropic oceanic climates** evaluates high in all these factors, making it the most vulnerable environment for corrosion. Deserts and Arctic regions are generally much less corrosive.

## Identifying and Preventing Corrosion
Different materials exhibit different signs of corrosive attack.
- On **aluminium and magnesium** structures (highly susceptible to salt), corrosion usually appears as a **white/grey powder**. It can also create "pitting" (white spots) which is extremely damaging to the structure.
- On **ferrous alloys** (iron-based metals), corrosion appears as rust colored **red to dark reddish-brown stains**. Some examiners also consider the discoloration of paint around these areas to be a reliable sign.

To combat corrosion, operators must maintain a constant cycle of cleaning, inspection, operational preservation, and lubrication. For aircraft operating near marine environments, the best course of action is to **wash the aircraft thoroughly and frequently**—including vulnerable parts like the landing gear and engines—and to cover up engines to wash away acidic contaminants and salt deposits. Replacing whole fleets with composite material aircraft would eliminate rust, but this is incredibly expensive and not feasible for many large jet operations.

## Corrosive Substances and Stress Corrosion
It is vital to be aware of other substances that can corrode aircraft:
- **Mercury** and **Battery Acid** are extremely corrosive to aluminium.
- **Water** slowly oxidizes unprotected metal.
- **Toilet Waste** contains moisture and ammonia, making it corrosive.
- **Hydraulic Fluids** are complex: they are generally non-corrosive to metals inside their systems, but can be highly corrosive to exterior paintwork, sealing compounds, and certain rubber materials. Additionally, they are skin and eye irritants and should never be mixed.
- **Fuel** itself is not corrosive, though bacteria can breed within it and cause tank corrosion if untreated.

Finally, **Stress Corrosion** is a highly dangerous condition resulting from the **combined effect of sustained tensile stresses and a corrosive environment**. It leads to premature failure and is particularly characteristic of aluminum, copper, stainless steels, and high-strength alloy steels.
