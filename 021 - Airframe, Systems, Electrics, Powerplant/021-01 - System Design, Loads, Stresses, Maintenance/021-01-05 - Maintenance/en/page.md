Aircraft maintenance is a multifaceted discipline dedicated to ensuring that aeroplanes remain reliable and airworthy. Different components age and wear in unique ways, meaning maintenance strategies must be carefully structured to prevent both expected and unforeseen failures.

## Types of Aircraft Maintenance

There are two main types of maintenance processes used to manage an aircraft's operational lifecycle:

### Hard-Time Maintenance
**Hard-time maintenance** refers to the planned, mandatory maintenance of a component. Under this process, a part must undergo a specific action—such as an overhaul, refurbishment, bench check, or complete replacement—at a **specific, pre-determined interval**. These intervals are based on the manufacturer's strict recommendations and are typically measured in:
- Flight hours
- Cycles of operation
- Calendar time

It is strictly forbidden to allow any component to remain in service after the conclusion of its hard-time period. 

### On-Condition Maintenance
**On-condition maintenance** is a form of predictive maintenance. Instead of removing a part at a fixed interval, maintenance teams perform **regular inspections and monitoring of critical parameters** on specific components or pieces of equipment. The component only receives maintenance (replacement or repair) if it falls out of condition or if a monitored limit value is exceeded.
These inspections are often designed so that engineers can quickly identify early indicators of wear or damage without completely tearing down each component, thus preventing failure **without excess work or unnecessary aircraft down-time**.

## Operational Realities and Aircraft Airworthiness 
Because different components wear at different rates, modern aircraft maintenance uses a combination of both philosophies. Some parts are subjected to hard-time maintenance, while others operate on-condition. 

When an aircraft is approved to fly after maintenance, it receives a **Certificate of Release to Service (CRS)**. This certificate denotes that the aircraft has been checked and is **considered airworthy until a defect is found or the specified time/cycle limits are reached**. 

However, flight intervals and ground times represent a complex situation. Large aircraft are built to fly almost continuously. If an aircraft spends a significant period parked (some operational sources say as little as a week), simply fulfilling standard flight-time limits or executing a fast visual pre-flight check is inadequate. It must be comprehensively inspected by an engineer (powered-up, engine runs, etc.) to confirm its airworthiness. Its airworthiness maintenance statement and records are updated during this process. A complex nuance relating to this fact is that the airworthiness maintenance statement might contain a "due date" (typically meaning a set number of future cycles, rather than a hard calendar date) outlining when further hard-time maintenance will be strictly necessary regardless of parking time.
