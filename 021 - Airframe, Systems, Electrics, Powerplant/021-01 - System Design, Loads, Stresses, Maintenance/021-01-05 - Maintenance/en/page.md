# Aircraft Maintenance Philosophies: Hard-Time and On-Condition

Aircraft maintenance is a critical process to ensure the continued airworthiness and safety of an aircraft. Maintenance programs are structured around specific philosophies that dictate when and how components should be inspected, repaired, or replaced. The two primary types of maintenance processes are **Hard-Time Maintenance** and **On-Condition Maintenance**.

## Hard-Time Maintenance

**Hard-time maintenance** refers to the planned maintenance of a component at specific, pre-determined intervals.

- **Triggers:** Action is required after a set number of **flight hours, cycles of operation, or calendar time** (due date).
- **Requirement:** Components must be **overhauled, refurbished, or replaced** once the limit is reached, per the manufacturer's recommendations.
- **Strict Limits:** It is strictly forbidden to allow components to remain in service after the conclusion of their hard-time period.

## On-Condition Maintenance

**On-condition maintenance** is a predictive maintenance process that involves the replacement or overhaul of components **before a failure happens**, based on their observed condition.

- **Method:** It relies on the **regular inspection** and **monitoring of critical parameters** of a component or piece of equipment.
- **Action:** Replacement occurs only if a specific **limit value is exceeded** or if a defect is found.
- **Advantages:** Inspections are often performed without completely tearing down components, allowing indicators of wear or damage to be identified quickly without excess work or aircraft down-time.

## Maintenance Validity and Parking

Aircraft typically utilize both hard-time and on-condition maintenance tasks depending on the component's wear characteristics and risk of failure.

- **Certificate of Release to Service (CRS):** If an aircraft has a CRS, it has been checked and is considered **airworthy until a defect is found or the specified time/cycle limits are reached**.
- **Parked Aircraft:** Large aircraft are designed to be flown almost continuously. If an aircraft is parked for any significant length of time, it must be **checked by an engineer** (subject to engine runs, fully powered-up, etc.) to inspect for airworthiness before returning to service.
