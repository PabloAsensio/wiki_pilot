Mountainous areas present unique challenges and significant hazards to aviation due to the interaction of terrain with the atmosphere.

## Orographic Effects on Meteorology
Mountains force air to rise (orographic lifting), which modifies weather systems:
*   **Windward Side**: Air rises, cools, and condenses. Clouds and precipitation form.
*   **Fronts**: The passage of a front through a mountainous area intensifies updrafts, which can hide Cumulonimbus (Cb) clouds within layers of stratiform clouds (**Embedded Cb**).

## Icing
The risk of icing is significantly higher in mountainous areas.
*   **Large Droplets**: Strong orographic lifting favors the formation of Supercooled Large Droplets (SLD).
*   **Clear Ice**: These large droplets tend to form **clear ice**, which is more dangerous and accumulates faster.
*   **Location**: The most severe icing usually occurs on the **windward** side and above the mountain ridge.

## Mountain Waves (Lee Waves)
These are oscillations of the air on the **leeward** side of a mountain range.
*   **Conditions for formation**:
    1.  Wind flowing perpendicular to the ridge (within 30°).
    2.  Wind speed at the summit > 20 knots and increasing with height.
    3.  Stable layer of air near the summit.
*   **Hazards**:
    *   **Vertical Currents**: Can exceed the aircraft's climb capability (up to 6000 fpm), pushing it towards the terrain.
    *   **Turbulence**: Severe Clear Air Turbulence (CAT).
    *   **Rotors**: Zone of extremely violent turbulence that forms below the wave crests (at mountain height). Often marked by **rotor clouds** (roll clouds).
*   **Lenticular Clouds**: Lens-shaped clouds that remain stationary over or downwind of the mountain. They are a clear visual indicator of mountain wave activity and severe turbulence.

### Flight Strategy
*   **Headwind towards the mountain**: You are approaching from the leeward side. Expect **downdrafts** and severe turbulence before crossing the ridge.
*   **Tailwind towards the mountain**: You are on the windward side. Expect updrafts before the ridge, but violent downdrafts immediately after crossing it.

## Katabatic Winds and Valley Inversions
*   **Katabatic Wind**: Flow of cold air descending mountain slopes at night due to radiation cooling.
*   **Valley Inversion**: Cold air accumulates at the bottom of the valley, creating a **cold air pool**.
    *   **Hazards**: Fog and poor visibility in the valley. Wind shear when exiting the inversion. Loss of performance when climbing into warmer air (inversion).
