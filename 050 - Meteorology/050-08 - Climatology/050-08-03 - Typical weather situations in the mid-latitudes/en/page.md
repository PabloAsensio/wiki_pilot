In the mid-latitudes (between 30° and 60°), the climate is dominated by the movement of pressure systems and fronts, influenced by the jet stream and the westerlies.

## Cold Air Drop (Cold Pool)

Also known as "Gota Fría" in Spanish contexts.

*   **Definition**: It is a depression (low-pressure system) that exists **only in the upper troposphere**. It consists of a pocket of air much colder than the surroundings at high levels, which has detached from the main polar air mass.
*   **Formation**: Occurs when cold air aloft separates from the polar region and moves towards lower latitudes (over warmer air).
*   **Detection**:
    *   **Visible on upper charts** (300 hPa, 500 hPa) as an area of low contours (low pressure).
    *   **NOT visible on surface charts**: At the surface, there may be high pressure or a flat pressure gradient, making detection difficult without analyzing upper layers.
*   **Associated Weather**:
    *   Generates **deep instability** (very cold air over warm surface air = large vertical temperature gradient).
    *   Causes strong convective activity: **Cumulonimbus**, severe thunderstorms, and heavy rains.
    *   Activity is maximum on summer afternoons over land (diurnal heating + cold air aloft).
*   **Prediction**: Difficult to forecast. Its movement is erratic, and numerical models sometimes fail to predict its exact track.
*   **Duration**: Typically about **3 days**, although they can persist for weeks if associated with static anticyclones (like the Siberian High).

## Westerly Waves

These are undulations in the general westerly flow corresponding to the movement of **polar front depressions** and their associated fronts.

*   **Movement**: From West to East.
*   **Location**: Mid-latitudes (40° - 60°).
*   **Weather**: Very variable ("changeable") weather. Alternation between the passage of fronts (rain, wind, clouds) and high-pressure ridges (dry and sunny weather).

## Polar Front Depressions

Low-pressure systems that form at the discontinuity between polar air and tropical air.

*   **Location**: Generally between 40°N and 65°N.
*   **Seasonal Displacement**:
    *   **Winter**: They shift **South** (towards the equator), pushed by the expansion of polar air. They are more intense due to the greater temperature contrast.
    *   **Summer**: They shift **North** (towards the pole). They are less intense.

## Westerlies

Prevailing winds blowing from the subtropical high-pressure belts towards the subpolar lows.

*   **Northern Hemisphere**: Stronger and more constant in winter (frequent gales in the North Atlantic). In summer, they are weaker.
*   **Southern Hemisphere**: Known as the **"Roaring Forties"** between 40°S and 50°S. They are very strong and constant throughout the year due to the absence of large landmasses to slow the flow.
