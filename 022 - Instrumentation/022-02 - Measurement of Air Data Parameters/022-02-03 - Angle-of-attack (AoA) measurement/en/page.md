The **Angle of Attack (AoA)** or alpha ($\alpha$) is the angle between the wing's chord line and the relative airflow. It is a critical parameter for stall warning and flight envelope protection.

## AoA Sensors
There are two primary types of sensors used to measure AoA:
1.  **Vane Detector:** An external vane (like a wind vane) that aligns itself with the relative airflow. Potentiometers measure its angle relative to the fuselage.
2.  **Slotted Probe (Conical):** A probe with two slots (upper and lower). Differential pressure between the slots causes internal paddles to rotate the probe until pressures equalize, aligning it with the airflow.

**Anti-Icing:** AoA probes are generally **electrically heated** to prevent ice formation which could freeze the vane/probe in a fixed position.

## Failure Modes
If an AoA sensor becomes **frozen/stuck**:
*   It will fail to detect changes in AoA.
*   The **Stall Warning System** may fail to activate even if the aircraft approaches a stall.
*   Flight Envelope protections based on alpha limits will be lost.

## System Outputs
AoA data is sent to the:
*   Air Data Computer (ADC)
*   Stall Warning System (Stick shaker/pusher)
*   Flight Envelope Protection System (e.g., Alpha Floor protection)
*   AoA Indicator (if fitted)
